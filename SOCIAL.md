## LinkedIn

There's a recursive trap at the center of AI context engineering that I haven't seen anyone name.

We build systems to manage LLM context limitations. Memory stores, retrieval layers, structured knowledge. The architecture for these systems exists in our heads. To codify it, we work through LLM conversations. Each conversation is context-limited. Each session captures a fragment. Across sessions, continuity depends on the memory infrastructure we haven't finished building.

The tool we're constructing to solve the problem is constrained by the problem during its own construction.

We went looking for the deeper problem. How do you bootstrap foundational knowledge into these systems when the extraction process itself is context-limited? What happens when multi-agent systems degrade... is there a threshold? We started mapping error thresholds from origin-of-life research onto context rot. Treating context management as a membrane function rather than a convenience feature.

We went looking for prior art. Autocatalytic closure theory applied to software architecture. Operational closure as a design principle. Eigen's error catastrophe mapped to LLM context degradation. The academic threads exist in isolation. The synthesis into something you can actually build and ship... that space is empty.

Biology solved the bootstrapping problem. DNA accreted through incremental mutation and selection. Each generation tested a small addition against reality and kept what survived. The specification grows by accretion. The organism provides selection pressure.

We're building the organism.
